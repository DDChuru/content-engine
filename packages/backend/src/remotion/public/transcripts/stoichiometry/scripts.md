## TikTok 1: "What Even IS a Mole?"
**Duration:** ~75 seconds | **Words:** ~190

You can't count atoms. They're way too small. So chemists invented something genius — the {{cue:mole}}.

A mole is just a number. A really, really big number. {{cue:six}} Six point zero two two times ten to the twenty-three. That's Avogadro's constant.

How big is that? If you had a mole of {{cue:rice}} rice grains, it would cover the entire Earth — mountains deep.

But here's why it's useful. {{cue:twelve}} Twelve grams of carbon-twelve contains exactly one mole of atoms. Twenty-three grams of sodium — one mole. Fifty-six grams of iron — also one mole.

See the pattern? The {{cue:mass}} mass number on the periodic table tells you exactly how many grams make one mole. That's called the molar mass.

So when a chemist says "give me one mole of water," they mean give me {{cue:eighteen}} eighteen grams. Because hydrogen is one, times two, plus oxygen sixteen — equals eighteen.

The mole connects what we can {{cue:weigh}} weigh on a scale to what's happening at the atomic level. That's the whole point. And once you get this, stoichiometry clicks.

## TikTok 2: "Molar Mass in 60 Seconds"
**Duration:** ~70 seconds | **Words:** ~180

The periodic table is literally a {{cue:cheat}} cheat sheet. Every single element has its molar mass written right there.

Carbon — {{cue:twelve}} twelve grams per mole. Oxygen — sixteen. Hydrogen — one. That's all you need to know.

But what about compounds? Easy. You just {{cue:add}} add them up.

Water — H two O. Hydrogen is one, times two that's two. Plus oxygen sixteen. {{cue:eighteen}} Eighteen grams per mole.

Carbon dioxide — CO two. Carbon twelve. Plus oxygen sixteen times two, that's thirty-two. {{cue:fortyfour}} Forty-four grams per mole.

Sulfuric acid — H two S O four. Hydrogen one times two — two. Sulfur — thirty-two. Oxygen sixteen times four — sixty-four. {{cue:ninetyeight}} Total? Ninety-eight grams per mole.

Here's the formula you need to {{cue:memorise}} memorise. Moles equals mass divided by molar mass. n equals m over big M.

You know two of those three things? You can find the third. That's it. That's the entire foundation of {{cue:stoichiometry}} stoichiometry calculations. Everything builds from here.

## TikTok 3: "The Mole Triangle"
**Duration:** ~70 seconds | **Words:** ~175

Every stoichiometry problem comes down to {{cue:one}} one formula. Moles equals mass divided by molar mass. n equals m over capital M.

And the easiest way to remember it? The {{cue:triangle}} triangle.

Draw a triangle. Put mass at the top. Moles bottom left. Molar mass bottom right. Cover what you want — and you can see the formula.

Want moles? Cover n. You see {{cue:mass-over}} mass over molar mass. Want mass? Cover m. You see moles times molar mass. Want molar mass? Cover M. Mass divided by moles.

Let's use it. {{cue:question}} Question — how many moles in twenty-four grams of magnesium?

Step one — molar mass of magnesium from the periodic table. {{cue:twentyfour}} Twenty-four grams per mole.

Step two — plug into the triangle. n equals twenty-four divided by twenty-four. {{cue:answer}} That's one mole. Done.

Another one. How many moles in {{cue:eleven}} eleven grams of CO two? Molar mass of CO two is forty-four. Eleven divided by forty-four equals {{cue:pointtwofive}} zero point two five moles.

Once you master this triangle, you can solve {{cue:any}} any mass-to-mole conversion. And that's most of your exam.
